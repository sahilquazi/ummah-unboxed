Item 01

Traditional Palestinian Kheffiyah

Woven on one of the last remaining mechanical looms in Hebron, Palestine. The black-and-white keffiyeh is more than a scarf — it's a symbol of Palestinian identity, resistance, and cultural continuity. Each one is made with cotton and features the iconic fishnet and olive leaf patterns that have been part of Palestinian dress for generations. This isn't mass-produced — it's made by families who have been weaving for decades.

Made by women artisans in cooperatives across Ramallah and Bethlehem, each piece takes several hours to complete. No two are exactly alike.

Item 03

Handmade Wooden Arabic Calligraphy Artwork

A one-of-a-kind piece of wall art, hand-burned into natural oak by a Palestinian woodworker in Bethlehem. The design features Arabic calligraphy intertwined with a hand-drawn flower motif — a visual reminder that beauty and faith are inseparable. Bethlehem has been a center for olive wood and hardwood carving for centuries, and this piece carries that tradition into contemporary Islamic home decor. Every burn mark is made by hand with a heated stylus — no machines, no shortcuts.

The Maker

He learned to carve wood before he learned to write. Now he does both at once.

Ibrahim Nasser grew up in Bethlehem, surrounded by olive wood workshops. His father carved nativity sets for export — a trade Bethlehem has been known for since the Ottoman period. But Ibrahim wanted to do something different. He wanted to burn Arabic script into wood — to turn verses, names, and prayers into art you could hang on a wall and touch with your hands.

He taught himself pyrography — the art of burning designs into wood using a heated metal tip. It requires absolute steadiness. One slip and the piece is ruined. He works on raw oak planks, drawing the calligraphy freehand in pencil before committing it to fire. The flower motifs he adds are his signature — inspired by the wildflowers that grow on the terraced hillsides outside Bethlehem every spring.

The piece in this month's box features Arabic calligraphy intertwined with a hand-drawn peony. Ibrahim describes it as "a prayer becoming a garden." Each plaque takes roughly six hours to complete. The burn depth, the line weight, the way the grain of the oak interacts with the design — all of it varies from piece to piece. No two are the same.

Since the economic blockade tightened, Ibrahim's local customer base has all but disappeared. International shipping is unreliable, and most craft export programs require minimum orders he can't meet alone. Ummah Unboxed gives him a channel to reach buyers in the US without middlemen taking the majority of the sale.

A Letter from Ibrahim

Translated from Arabic.
Included in every box.

Ibrahim Nasser · Bethlehem, West Bank · April 2026

I want you to know something about the piece of wood you are holding. It is oak — not olive, which is what everyone expects from Bethlehem. I chose oak because it is harder. It resists the burn. You have to be patient with it, and when the design finally appears, it feels earned. That is how I think about faith.

The calligraphy on your piece is not decorative. It is a prayer. I wrote it first in pencil, the way my father taught me to plan a carving — measure twice, cut once. Then I burned it in by hand, one stroke at a time. The flower beside it is a peony. They grow wild here in spring. I add one to every piece because I want people to remember that things still grow here.

My workshop is small. It is just me, a desk, and a window that looks out at the hills. I make these pieces one at a time. When I heard that someone in America would be holding this — reading this letter — I wanted you to know that it was made with intention. Every line.

Thank you for making it possible for me to keep working. That is not a small thing.

— Ibrahim
